Abstract
This study aims to explore the feasibility of utilizing a combination of original and delta cone-beam CT (CBCT) radiomics for predicting treatment response in liver tumors undergoing stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT). A total of 49 patients are included in this study, with 36 receiving 5-fraction SBRT, 3 receiving 4-fraction SBRT, and 10 receiving 3-fraction SBRT. The CBCT and planning CT images from liver cancer patients who underwent SBRT are collected to extract overall 547 radiomics features. The CBCT features which are reproducible and interchangeable with pCT are selected for modeling analysis. The delta features between fractions are calculated to depict tumor change. The patients with 4-fraction SBRT are only used for screening robust features. In patients receiving 5-fraction SBRT, the predictive ability of both original and delta CBCT features for two-level treatment response (local efficacy vs. local non-efficacy; complete response (CR) vs. partial response (PR)) is assessed by utilizing multivariable logistic regression with leave-one-out cross-validation. Additionally, univariate analysis is conducted to validate the capability of CBCT features in identifying local efficacy in patients receiving 3-fraction SBRT. In patients receiving 5-fraction SBRT, the combined models incorporating original and delta CBCT radiomics features demonstrate higher area under the curve (AUC) values compared to models using either original or delta features alone for both classification tasks. The AUC values for predicting local efficacy vs. local non-efficacy are 0.58 for original features, 0.82 for delta features, and 0.90 for combined features. For distinguishing PR from CR, the respective AUC values for original, delta and combined features are 0.79, 0.80, and 0.89. In patients receiving 3-fraction SBRT, eight valuable CBCT radiomics features are identified for predicting local efficacy. The combination of original and delta radiomics derived from fractionated CBCT images in liver cancer patients undergoing SBRT shows promise in providing comprehensive information for predicting treatment response.

Introduction
Liver cancer is the most common abdominal tumor, including primary hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) and metastases from various malignancies, such as colorectal, lung, and breast cancers [1]. For liver metastasis, 80–90% of patients are not resectable at diagnosis. Thus it was once considered an incurable disease with a poor prognosis [2]. Currently, stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT) has demonstrated efficacy in the ablation of liver tumors and metastases, with reported 1-year local control (LC) rates ranging from 71 to 97.4%, and 2-year rates ranging from 71 to 93% [3,4,5,6,7].

Assessment of treatment response is crucial for patients undergoing SBRT to develop appropriate treatment plans post-SBRT. However, in routine clinical settings, the traditional method of evaluating the efficacy of therapy often takes several months, which results in a delayed assessment for patients with negative outcomes. For instance, commonly employed methods for assessing treatment response, such as Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) and modified RECIST (mRECIST), rely on measuring the tumor diameter on follow-up CT or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) [8]. These methods are not suitable for early intervention [9]. There is a significant clinical need for a new method that allows rapid assessment of treatment response and timely adjustment of treatment regimens in response to treatment effects.

For liver tumor patients who received SBRT, daily online cone-beam CT (CBCT) is routinely performed for set-up correction and position verification at every fraction to guarantee the high-precise radiotherapy to the targeted lesions and the safety of organs at risk (OAR) [10,11,12,13,14]. CBCT images provide valuable information on the daily changes in tumors during treatment, despite the poor image quality [15]. Previous studies have shown that some CBCT radiomics features can be repeatedly obtained from CBCT images. Therefore, CBCT radiomics features have the potential to be used as a tumor evaluation metric [16]. Timmeren et al. [17] found that some radiomics features extracted from CBCT are interchangeable with those in planning CT (pCT). CBCT radiomics can be used as a prognostic imaging biomarker for patients with non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). It provides an idea for assessing the treatment response of liver SBRT patients on fractionated CBCT images through radiomics. In our previous study, delta radiomics features obtained from CT images of different treatment stages were found to be more predictive than those obtained at a single time in the evaluation of the neoadjuvant chemotherapy response of high-grade osteosarcoma [18]. The delta-radiomics concept is also reported to help predict treatment outcomes in rectal cancer [19], pancreatic cancer [20], and locally advanced lung cancer [21]. The fractionated nature of CBCT scans in SBRT makes them capable of assessing the dynamic change of the tumor. The delta CBCT radiomics features might have higher performance than the original features in treatment response prediction. However, to our knowledge, no previous studies have examined the feasibility of predicting efficacy using original and delta CBCT radiomics in patients with liver cancer treated with SBRT.

Therefore, we propose the hypothesis that the CBCT radiomics approach could assist clinicians in identifying treatment responses in patients with liver tumors who received SBRT and that the delta-CBCT radiomics features have a higher predictive value than the original features. Firstly, we investigate the interchangeability of radiomics features extracted from pCT and CBCT. Then we analyze the dynamic changes in the stable radiomics features of CBCT during the treatment. Finally, we evaluate and compare the predictive value of original, delta, and combined CBCT radiomics features to help clinicians evaluate the treatment response and make appropriate clinical strategies for liver cancer SBRT.

Materials and methods
Study design
The workflow of this study is shown in Fig. 1. This study mainly includes four parts: Tumor Segmentation, Feature Extraction, Feature Selection, and Data Analysis. We collect the pCT and CBCT images of liver cancer patients treated with SBRT. The gross tumor volume (GTV) in the patient radiotherapy plan is used as the volume of interest (VOI) of pCT. The VOI on CBCT is acquired by performing registration from pCT to each fraction of CBCT. All the VOIs on CBCT are manually checked and adjusted. Radiomics features are then extracted from the VOIs to quantify the tumor heterogeneity. Radiomics features include intensity-based, grey level co-occurrence matrix (GLCM) based features, grey level run length matrix (GLRLM) based features, grey level size zone matrix (GLSZM) based features, neighborhood grey tone difference matrix (NGTDM) based features, wavelet-transform (Wavelet) based features and shape-based features. The CBCT radiomics features are screened based on their robustness to VOI segmentation uncertainties, correlation with tumor volume, and interchangeability with pCT-derived features. Subsequently, the delta CBCT radiomics features are calculated to characterize the relative tumor change within neighboring fractions and compared to the baseline fraction. Then, the ability of original and delta CBCT radiomics features to identify treatment responses is assessed in patients treated with 5-fraction SBRT using the leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOCV) approach. Considering the limited patient population in the 3-fraction and 4-fraction SBRT cohorts, we exclusively employ images from patients undergoing 4-fraction SBRT for feature selection purposes. Additionally, we utilize images from patients treated with 3-fraction SBRT solely for univariate analysis. This analysis aims to determine whether both the original and delta CBCT radiomics features possess the capability to predict patient response within the 3-fraction SBRT treatment group.

Volume-of-interest delineation
The GTV segmentation on the pCT image in the radiotherapy plan is performed manually and serves as the volume of interest (VOI) for the radiomics analysis. To align the GTV on the pCT with the CBCT image, we employ the “Standard Deformable Image Alignment (DIR) algorithm” provided by SmartAdapt® (Varian Medical Systems, version 11.0) [22, 23]. The registration process is conducted using the pCT and CBCT images acquired during each treatment fraction. To ensure the reliability of the VOIs, two experienced radiation therapists manually review and adjust all VOIs. In cases where a patient has multiple tumors, the VOI corresponding to the tumor with the largest diameter is selected [24]. Uncertainty in tumor delineation could introduce potential errors in the calculated radiomics features since they are derived from the delineated VOIs. To address this concern and identify robust radiomics features that are less sensitive to uncertainty-driven segmentation, we simulate two additional sets of VOIs. This is achieved by performing expansion and erosion operations on a randomly selected slice from the original VOI. By examining the stability and consistency of radiomics features across these different VOI sets, robustness against uncertainties associated with tumor delineation can be used to select reliable features for subsequent analyses.

Feature extraction
All pCT and CBCT VOIs are resampled into the same voxel size of 
 mm3 using cubic interpolation [25]. All images are normalized into eight gray levels to prevent sparsely populated matrices, as reported in the study of Xenia Fave et al. [16]. This is done to normalize the different pixel widths and the slice thickness of CBCT and pCT VOIs, as well as decrease the influence of motion blur and prescribed dose on CBCT images before feature extraction [21]. We extract 547 radiomics features of the pCT and CBCT VOIs on each patient, respectively, which is performed using Matlab 2017a (MathWorks, Natick, Massachusetts, USA) with the radiomics features extraction package provided by M Vallières et al. [26]. Feature names and corresponding abbreviations can be found in our previous study [27]. The prefixes ‘CBCT1’, ‘CBCT2’, ‘CBCT3’, ‘CBCT4’, and ‘CBCT5’ are used to label the radiomics feature derived from the CBCT images scanned in different fractions. For example, the feature ‘CBCT5_HHH_Histogram_Entropy’ represents the histogram-based entropy features derived from the VOI transformed by performing a high-pass wavelet filter on all three axes from CBCT images scanned in the 5th fraction.

Features selection
The multi-step procedure for the selection of robust and representative CBCT radiomics features is performed to improve the reproducibility of radiomics analysis. Firstly, the robustness of radiomics features to uncertainty-driven tumor segmentation is assessed using the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) to measure the agreement between radiomics features derived from different VOI contours (original VOI and two simulated VOIs). Features with an ICC value higher than 0.75 that are considered good reproducibility are selected [28]. Secondly, since the radiomics features that are highly correlated with tumor volume could not provide additional information [21], the radiomics features with a high correlation with tumor volume are excluded. The correlation between selected features and tumor volume is analyzed using the Spearman correlation analysis method, and the p-value in the correlation analysis is corrected using the Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) approach [29]. The features with a corrected p-value higher than 0.05 (95% confidence interval) are retained. Finally, we perform a correlation analysis between the radiomics features of the baseline CBCT images (CBCT-1) and pCT images to assess the interchangeability between CBCT and pCT radiomics features. In this procedure, the Spearman correlation test is used to assess the correlation between each radiomics feature of CBCT-1 and pCT images. A corrected p-value of less than 0.05 is used to select interchangeable CBCT radiomics features in the Spearman correlation test. Only those radiomics features that show a significant correlation between CBCT-1 images and pCT images are used in the following analysis.

CBCT radiomics features for identifying treatment response
The tumor response evaluation criteria mRECIST is used to classify treatment response into four groups defined as (1) CR: complete disappearance; (2) PR: at least 30% reduction in the sum of target lesion diameters (arterial phase enhancement); (3) PD: at least 20% increase in the sum of target lesion diameters (enhancement); (4) SD: little change in size or virtually unchanged. To assess the ability of CBCT radiomics to classify tumor response, a two-tiered process is defined, with the first tier classifying locally effective (PR&CR) and locally ineffective (PD&SD) patients, followed by a second tier separating PR patients from CR patients to identify adjustments in early treatment strategies for PR patients.

Considering the characteristics of a small sample size and an unbalanced distribution of patients’ response type in SBRT of liver cancer (more patients with local efficacy or PR in clinic), the LOOCV method and over-under resampling approach is used to evaluate the recognition ability of CBCT radiomics features to patients’ responses [30]. Leave-one-out-cross-validation (LOOCV) is a widely used technique in machine learning and statistical analysis to assess the performance and generalization ability of a predictive model. In LOOCV, each data point in the dataset is sequentially removed or “left out” as a test sample, while the remaining data points are used to train the model. The process is repeated for each data point, resulting in as many rounds of training, and testing as there are data points in the dataset. This approach allows us to evaluate the model’s performance on unseen data and estimate its ability to generalize well. For each train-and-validation process, the over-under sampling approach is used for the training model to obtain a relatively balanced dataset. The oversampling is performed with the bootstrap approach (package “ROSE” in R). For the category with fewer samples, the over-sampling method is utilized; for the category with more samples, the under-resampling method is performed. The maximum Relevance Minimum Redundancy (mRMR) approach [31], combined with multivariable logistic regression, is used to select important radiomics features for model construction. Three types of models are constructed for comparison, which are the model using CBCT radiomics features only, the model using delta-radiomics features only, and the model using both original and delta CBCT radiomics features. The receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve and the area under the ROC curve (AUC) are used to evaluate the performance of predicting tumor responses of CBCT radiomics features. The Log-rank test is used to compare the AUC between different models.

Univariate analysis in patients with 3-fraction SBRT
To evaluate the prediction value of CBCT radiomics features in patients with 3-fraction SBRT, this part of the population is treated as an independent subgroup due to the potential influence of different fraction numbers on outcomes. The Mann-Whitney U test is used to assess the ability of each feature to classify PR&CR (local efficacy) and PD&SD (local non-efficacy) patients in patients with 3-fraction SBRT. The feature with a p-value smaller than 0.05 is considered to have good classification ability. The distribution of the selected radiomics features in the local efficacy group and the local non-efficacy group is visually presented using box plots. Freedom from local progression (FFLP) is defined as freedom from any progression within the planning target volume (PTV). The FFLP analysis is performed utilizing the Kaplan–Meier method. All statistical analyses are performed using R software version 3.6.0 (http://www.r-project.org).

All CBCT and pCT VOIs of the 49 patients are used for the selection of robust and representative CBCT radiomics features. Among the 547 CBCT radiomics features, 145 radiomics features are screened, which are robust to uncertainty-driven tumor segmentation and uncorrelated with tumor volume. Finally, we find that eleven CBCT radiomics features are interchangeable with pCT. These features are all wavelet-based radiomics features containing two LLL-wavelet features (LLL_GLCM_autoc, LLL_GLCM_ energ), two LHL-wavelet features (LHL_GLCM_energ, LHL_GLCM_senth), two LHH-wavelet features (LHH_Intensity_Skewness, LHH_GLCM_energ), one HLL-wavelet feature (HLL_GLCM_entro), one HLH-wavelet feature (HLH_GLCM_energ), one HHL-wavelet feature (HHL_GLCM_energ), two HHH-wavelet features (HHH_GLCM_energ, HHH_GLSZM_GLV). CBCT radiomics features for identifying response to treatment
The LOOCV analysis shows good performance of CBCT radiomics both in predicting local efficacy (PR&CR vs. PD&SD) and identifying PR patients from CR patients. The models constructed by combining original and delta CBCT radiomics features yield higher AUC than those using original or delta features only in both the two-level classification tasks. For predicting local efficacy, the feature number used for model construction in the LOOCV is tested from three to seven. For the model using CBCT radiomics features only, the five-feature model shows the highest LOOCV-AUC value (0.58, 95% CI: 0.36–0.80), as shown in Fig. 2a. For the delta-CBCT model, the model constructed with three features has the highest LOOCV-AUC (0.82, 95% CI: 0.67–0.98), as shown in Fig. 2b. For the combined model, the model constructed with three features exhibits the highest LOOCV-AUC (0.90, 95% CI: 0.79-1), as shown in Fig. 2c. The ROC curves of the three models are shown in Fig. 3a. The log-rank test reveals a significant improvement in the AUC when comparing the original CBCT model to the combined model (p-value = 0.01), while no significant difference is observed between the delta-CBCT model and the combined model (p-value = 0.14) or between the delta-CBCT model and the original CBCT model (p-value = 0.07). The three most important CBCT radiomics features in the LOOCV process of constructing the combined model are Delta_CBCT_2_1_LLH_GLCM_energ, Delta_CBCT_4_1_HHH_GLCM_energ, and Delta_CBCT_4_1_LLL_GLCM_autoc. These three features all represent the relative change of tumor on CBCT during SBRT; for example, the Delta_CBCT_2_1_LLH_GLCM_energ is the relative change of the feature LLH_GLCM_energ in the 2nd fraction of CBCT images compared to this feature derived from the baseline CBCT (1st fraction). The result indicates the high potential of delta-CBCT radiomics features that characterize the tumor change during SBRT in predicting patient local efficacy.

Univariate analysis in patients with 3-fraction SBRT
In parallel to the study of the 5-fraction prediction model, by performing univariate analysis on the datasets of patients with 3-fraction SBRT, eight CBCT radiomics features are found distinct between the local efficacy group and non-local efficacy group, as shown in Fig. 4. The eight features include two original CBCT radiomics features and six delta-CBCT features. The feature names are explained as follows:

a): CBCT2_LHL_GLCM_senth: a specific measure of wavelet-based texture feature LHL_GLCM_senth in the second fraction of CBCT images. The “senth-” is the sum entropy of GLCM. The LHL- represents the category of features derived from CBCT decomposed by low-pass wavelet transform on the X-axis, high-pass wavelet transforms on the Y-axis and low-pass wavelet transform on the Z-axis.

b): Delta_fea_2_1_LHL_GLCM_senth: the relative change in the LHL_GLCM_senth feature between the second and first fractions of CBCT images, where LHL_GLCM_senth is a wavelet-based texture feature derived from GLCM.

c): Delta_fea_3_1_HHH_GLCM_energ: the relative change in the HHH_GLCM_energ feature between the third and first fractions of CBCT images, where HHH_GLCM_energ is a wavelet-based feature derived from GLCM. The “energ” is the energy of GLCM.

d): Delta_fea_3_1_LHH_GLCM_energ: the relative change in the LHH_GLCM_energ feature between the third and first fractions of CBCT images, where LHH_GLCM_energ is yet another wavelet-based feature derived from GLCM analysis.

e): CBCT3_LHH_GLCM_energ: a specific measure of wavelet-based texture feature LHH_GLCM_energ in the third fraction of CBCT images.

f): Delta_fea_3_2_Volume: the relative change in tumor volume between the third and second fractions of CBCT images;

g): Delta_fea_3_1_LHL_GLCM_senth: the relative change in the LHL_GLCM_senth feature between the third and first fractions of CBCT images.

h): Delta_fea_3_1_HLH_GLCM_energ: the relative change in the HLH_GLCM_energ feature between the third and first fractions of CBCT images, where HLH_GLCM_energ is also a wavelet-based texture feature derived from GLCM.

Among these features, the wavelet-based GLCM_energ and its delta feature are also identified as important in predicting local efficacy and distinguishing PR patients in 5-fraction SBRT treatment groups.

Discussion
In this pilot study, we demonstrate the potential of CBCT radiomics as a tool for evaluating the response of liver cancer patients to SBRT treatment. Our LOOCV process reveals that a combination of original and delta radiomics features yields an AUC of 0.90 (95% CI: 0.79-1) for predicting local efficacy (PR&CR vs. PD&SD), and an AUC of 0.89 (95% CI: 0.77-1) for PR and CR patient classification. The combined model containing robust original and delta CBCT radiomics features not only captures tumor changes during treatment but also outperforms either the original or delta CBCT radiomics model alone in predicting patient response. The significance observed in the log-rank test further supports the superiority of the combined model compared to the original CBCT model, indicating that the inclusion of delta features contributes to the overall accuracy of the model. Moreover, the CBCT radiomics features are also found to be useful in predicting local efficacy in patients with 3-fraction SBRT, further highlighting the predictive ability of CBCT radiomics in assessing treatment response in liver cancer patients receiving SBRT. Our results suggest that the CBCT images scanned during SBRT could provide valuable information for clinicians to predict patient response, serving as an early biomarker for treatment response. Our study also provides a basis for further evaluation of the clinical utility of CBCT radiomics.

When compared to similar studies, it is evident that there is a lack of research specifically focused on CBCT radiomics in liver cancer patients undergoing SBRT. In the context of liver cancer, several studies have explored the role of radiomics in predicting treatment response to different therapies, including SBRT. In most radiomics studies, CT or MRI scans are only performed at baseline or a very limited number of points in time, hampering the possibility of timely treatment adaptation [17]. For instance, Cozzi et al. [32] revealed the ability of pCT radiomics to predict local response and overall survival for patients with HCC in a cohort of 138 patients, but they did not analyze patients treated with SBRT separately. Similarly, Ho et al. [33] investigated the predictive value of pre-treatment MRI in unresectable HCC patients following novel sequential TACE-SBRT-immunotherapy in a cohort of 26 patients, which cannot depict the dynamic change of tumors during SBRT.

The longitudinal approach with the change of quantitative radiomics features may contribute to the response assessment compared to baseline images [17, 34, 35]. CBCT images are generally acquired daily for treatment positioning verification, which makes this ideal for longitudinal treatment response assessment with radiomics [36]. Considering the single-time CBCT radiomics feature can’t characterize the tumor change during SBRT, we incorporate the delta CBCT radiomics features. The results demonstrate both the original and delta CBCT radiomics features contribute to identifying response to the treatment and combining the original and delta features for analysis had stronger prediction power. Jin et al. [37] identified the high potential of predicting local control in liver lesions using longitudinal MRI-guided SBRT radiomics features in a cohort of 22 patients. However, as MR-guided SBRT is not yet widely applied, prediction methods based on longitudinal MRI images will be hampered in SBRT. While MRI may be superior to CBCT in soft tissue resolution, CBCT has been more widely used in the SBRT patient position before treatment. Jin’s finding of the prediction value of longitudinal radiomics in SBRT is in accordance with our study. This current study contributes to the existing literature by specifically examining the performance of CBCT radiomics in predicting treatment response and differentiating treatment outcomes in liver cancer patients undergoing SBRT. The findings highlight the potential of CBCT radiomics features and emphasize the importance of combining both original and delta features for optimal predictive capability. It provides valuable insights into the use of CBCT radiomics as a potential tool for personalized treatment optimization and decision-making in liver cancer patients.

The suboptimal quality of CBCT images, coupled with the relatively lengthy scanning time of up to 1 min, significantly increases the likelihood of motion artifacts, thereby limiting the quantitative applicability of CBCT in clinical settings [15, 17]. Extensive scattering and metallic artifacts around implanted particles are other potential concerns of CBCT. To mitigate these issues, we have excluded CBCT images exhibiting severe scatter or motion/metal artifacts to ensure minimal impact on the tumor region’s intensity. Nonetheless, through meticulous preprocessing and robust feature selection techniques, CBCT images can still be effectively employed for quantitative radiomics analysis, as evidenced by previous studies. Notably, Fave et al. [16] have demonstrated the robustness and reproducibility of certain radiomics features extracted from CBCT images. Additionally, Bagher-Ebadian et al. have indicated that radiomics features derived from both pCT and daily CBCT image datasets for head/neck cancer patients remain resilient even under conditions of low-power Gaussian noise and low-pass filtering. In our study, we initially confirmed the interchangeability of pCT and liver CBCT radiomics features, aligning with similar research findings in non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) patients [16, 17]. Although our multi-procedure feature selection approach guarantees the reliability of radiomics features, it would be advantageous to establish quantitative requirements for CBCT image quality in future investigations. By identifying specific metrics or indices that objectively assess image quality, clearer guidelines can be formulated, enhancing the efficient utilization of our methodology across diverse clinical scenarios.

To explore the potential influence of treatment history on the response to SBRT, we conducted an additional investigation into the correlation between pre-SBRT therapy and response type in patients with 5-fraction SBRT. In our study, a total of 14 patients received different forms of therapy such as chemotherapy, target therapy, trans-arterial chemoembolization (TACE), or their combination before undergoing SBRT. The remaining 21 patients did not receive any additional treatment before SBRT. Among those who underwent pre-SBRT therapy, 12 patients exhibited local efficacy after SBRT, while 2 patients experienced local non-efficacy. Conversely, among patients without pre-SBRT therapy, 15 patients showed local efficacy, and 6 patients displayed local non-efficacy. However, statistical analysis using the chi-square test indicates no significant difference (p-value = 0.331) between patients with and without pre-SBRT therapy. It is important to emphasize that our analysis specifically focuses on the potential effect of whether patients receive additional therapy before starting SBRT. Nevertheless, it is worth noting that the specific type of pre-SBRT therapy administered may still have a potential impact on the treatment response. Therefore, further exploration involving larger sample sizes is warranted to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the potential effects of different pre-SBRT therapy types.

For clinical response assessment, traditional response assessment relies on periodic liver-enhanced MRI or CT examinations and the use of RECIST or the European Association for the Study of the Liver (EASL) as assessment criteria several months after SBRT [38]. These methods usually take months and are not suitable for early management interventions. In addition, a range of clinical tools has been developed to predict local control or survival after SBRT. For example, higher BED and smaller tumor volume are associated with better local control and survival [39,40,41]. Franceschini et al. uncover the relationship between the presence of lung metastases or nodal metastases related to a longer OS for oligometastatic patients treated with SBRT [42]. Franzese et al. conclude that the time from diagnosis of metastases to SBRT (HR 1.62, p = 0.02) is the prediction factor of local control [43]. The classification thresholds for each indicator are still not uniform. In addition, the specific stereotactic radiation dose is influenced by many factors, such as tumor location, the organ at risk, the volume or diameter of the tumor, control of non-targeted lesions, or the general situation of patients, etc. All these factors brought great challenges for each clinical indicator to find the cut-off value predicting survival after SBRT. There have also been studies using imaging tools such as 18F-FDG-PET/CT to predict local control, which still needs further confirmation [44, 45].

As the existing CBCT imaging data are available for every patient receiving SBRT, the findings of this study demonstrate the feasibility of using cost-effective radiomics techniques to predict treatment response. In addition to being low-cost, and reducing radiation exposure to patients, CBCT images could potentially assess the treatment response in advance compared with conventional following-up diagnosed contrast-enhanced CT or MRI and assist clinicians in intervening early for a certain subset of patients. The use of our model can contribute to the emerging field of personalized medicine. By leveraging the wealth of information provided by CBCT radiomics, clinicians can tailor treatment strategies based on individual patient characteristics, potentially improving treatment outcomes, and minimizing unnecessary side effects. Sorafenib, TACE, or a checkpoint inhibitor could also be intervened earlier for patients without response even progressing after SBRT based on CBCT radiomics. Our model holds the potential to serve as a clinical decision-support tool. By integrating it into existing clinical systems, clinicians can receive automated predictions and risk assessments, aiding in treatment decision-making and optimizing dose distribution for subsequent treatment. Additionally, these findings highlight the potential for future research and collaboration to refine and validate our model further in clinical settings.

Radiomics analysis with liver CBCT images has a few potential challenges. The biggest obstacle to the routine use of CBCT in clinical evaluation comes from the poor image quality due to artifacts inherent to the CBCT method. These artifacts can be mitigated by using an iterative reconstruction-based approach that reconstructs the original volume from the CBCT projection data. In patients with liver cancer, CBCT images are more susceptible to motion artifacts due to long scan times. 4D-CBCT imaging may overcome this problem but is accompanied by degraded image quality and increased noise [46]. 4D-CBCT is not yet commonly used in clinical practice. We have proposed a deep-learning-based method to obtain 4D-CBCT images from routine scans, which improves the image quality of CBCT [47]. Further studies will be conducted to investigate the effectiveness of improved CBCT image quality in clinical applications.

The imaging parameters might have a potential impact on the generalizability of this study, specifically in the context of CBCT and pCT. While we have employed resampling and normalization techniques to mitigate variations, it is important to recognize that these adjustments may not eliminate all discrepancies. Imaging parameters, such as scan settings, and reconstruction algorithms, can vary across different imaging systems and institutions, potentially influencing the radiomics features extracted. Therefore, the generalizability of our findings to other settings with different imaging parameters should be interpreted with caution. In addition, during the delineation of the VOI on CBCT images, it is common practice for the size of the VOI to be intentionally smaller than the GTV defined on pCT images. This adjustment is made to ensure accurate localization of the tumor on CBCT images across different treatment fractions. It is worth noting that the omission of surrounding tumor areas on CBCT images may also impact the ability to assess tumor response, as changes in these areas can provide valuable insights into treatment efficacy.

One limitation of our study is the relatively small sample size. It is essential to acknowledge that a small cohort increases the possibility of biased or overestimated outcomes, limiting the generalizability of our findings to a broader population. To address the concern of overfitting and ensure the reliability of our analysis, we implemented several measures. Firstly, we perform strict feature selection using the mRMR approach before constructing our model. This helped us identify the most informative and relevant features while minimizing redundancy. Additionally, during both model construction and evaluation, we employ LOOCV to obtain a rigorous estimate of model performance and reduce the potential impact of the small sample size. Our methodology has been successfully applied in a previous study with a similar sample size [48]. Furthermore, subsequent studies [49, 50] utilizing larger datasets and multiple healthcare centers have demonstrated the validity and generalizability of cross-validation. We also perform a sample size estimation [51] calculation to ensure the statistical robustness of our findings for patients who underwent 5-fraction SBRT. Based on the local non-efficacy rates observed in our study, we estimated that a sample size of 22 patients (17 with local efficacy and 5 with local non-efficacy) would be sufficient to achieve a power value of 0.8 for the AUC of the combined model. Furthermore, we found that a sample size of 20 patients (15 with PR and 5 with CR) would be sufficient to validate the results for patients with PR&CR after SBRT. These results suggest that our reported findings are supported by an adequate sample size to ensure their statistical robustness. Moreover, due to the different sample sizes, different analysis methods are applied to the patients treated with 3-fraction and 5-fraction SBRT. The patients with 3-fraction are used further to confirm the prognostic value of CBCT radiomics features with univariate analysis only. Even though the CBCT radiomics approach has demonstrated potential for predicting patient outcomes, adaptive modeling methods should be applied to patients with different fractionated treatments.

Despite our joint efforts in screening and data collection at the clinic, only a few patients qualify for inclusion. The specific inclusion and exclusion criteria employed in our study, along with other factors such as the availability of certain resources or time constraints, might have contributed to the small number of eligible patients. These factors could potentially restrict the broader application of our findings to a larger patient population. Our study serves as an initial exploration or pilot investigation of the proposed method or approach. The small number of eligible patients reflects the challenges inherent in recruiting a homogeneous patient population meeting the specific criteria of our study design. Therefore, this study serves as a pilot investigation, and further studies with a larger and more diverse patient cohort are warranted to validate our results and assess the robustness of radiomics analysis more comprehensively.

Conclusion
In this study, we conducted feature extraction from CBCT and pCT images of liver tumor patients undergoing SBRT. We observe that some radiomics features derived from both pCT and CBCT are interchangeable, indicating their comparable usefulness. Our experimental results demonstrate the potential of CBCT radiomics features, as well as their changes over time, to serve as early biomarkers for identifying treatment responses. Furthermore, by combining the original and delta radiomics features extracted from fractionated CBCT images, we can obtain more comprehensive information for predicting treatment response in liver cancer patients undergoing SBRT. This suggests that the integration of these complementary radiomics measures can enhance the accuracy and reliability of response prediction. Overall, our findings highlight the valuable role of CBCT radiomics features in assessing treatment response in liver cancer patients. Leveraging these features, alongside their dynamic changes, provides a promising avenue for early identification and monitoring of treatment outcomes in SBRT.
